# Example: Building a Prospective Practice

> This is an illustrative example based on composite real-world patterns. The characters and organizations are fictional. The situations are not.

## Context

The leadership team of a regional healthcare services organization had a recurring problem. Every eighteen months or so, something would change in their operating environment and they would spend three to four months in reactive mode: adjusting strategy, reallocating resources, and explaining to their board why they had not seen it coming.

They always had seen it coming, in a sense. The signals had been there. Someone on the front line had mentioned it in a meeting. Someone had read something relevant and sent it around by email. But there was no practice for taking those signals seriously before they became crises.

The CEO, Renata, introduced Pillar 6 of Kasvu not as a planning tool but as a conversation practice. Her premise was simple: the organization already had the information it needed to anticipate change. It just did not have a habit of listening to it.

## What she did

### Name the pattern honestly

Renata started by naming the reactive cycle in a leadership team meeting without blame.

She said: "We keep getting surprised by things we already knew. I want to understand why and build something that changes it."

She then asked each person on the leadership team to name one signal they were currently seeing in their environment that had not yet made it into a strategic conversation. Every person in the room had at least one. Several overlapped.

That exercise alone was clarifying. The information existed. The channel did not.

### Build the Prospective dialogue into the rhythm

Renata introduced a standing fifteen-minute segment at the end of every monthly leadership meeting called "Where does the wind blow to?"

The format was simple. Each leader arrived with one answer to one of three questions:

- What are we seeing more of that we were not seeing six months ago?
- What is one thing a customer, patient, or front-line staff member said recently that stayed with you?
- What is one thing changing in our environment that we have not yet named as a team?

No slides. No preparation beyond paying attention during the month. Just a conversation.

### Connect front-line signals to leadership

Renata recognized that the leadership team's peripheral vision was limited by their altitude. The people closest to patients and operational realities were seeing things the leadership team was not.

She introduced a monthly signal-sharing practice at the team leader level, one level below the leadership team. Team leaders were asked to share one observation per month through a shared document that the leadership team reviewed before their Prospective segment.

Within three months the quality of the Prospective conversations changed significantly. Instead of discussing macro trends the leadership team had read about, they were discussing specific, concrete signals from inside the organization and its immediate environment.

### Let Prospective inform Experimentation

The most important structural change Renata made was connecting the output of the Prospective conversations to the organization's experimentation pathway.

When a signal appeared consistently across two or three monthly conversations, it became a candidate for a small experiment rather than a strategic planning item. This kept the response proportionate to the certainty of the signal and prevented the organization from over-investing in trends that turned out to be noise.

## What worked

The fifteen-minute standing segment was the right size. Longer and it would have competed with operational priorities. Shorter and it would not have had room to go anywhere useful.

Connecting front-line signals to leadership conversations was the change with the highest impact. The organization had been making strategic decisions with incomplete sensory information. The signal-sharing practice fixed that without requiring a significant investment of time or resources.

## What was harder than expected

The first three months of the Prospective segment were uneven. Some leaders arrived with sharp, specific observations. Others arrived with vague generalities. Renata solved this by sharing an example of a strong signal versus a weak signal at the start of the fourth session, and the quality improved immediately.

The bigger challenge was patience. Prospective thinking produces its value slowly and invisibly. It is hard to point to a crisis that did not happen. Renata had to be deliberate about naming when the practice paid off, connecting the dots publicly so the team could see the return on their attention.

## What Renata would do differently

She would have brought team leaders into the practice sooner. She waited three months before introducing the signal-sharing document, partly out of caution and partly because she wanted to establish the leadership team's own rhythm first. In hindsight that delay cost her the best signals. The people closest to the work were always the most valuable source. She would build the two layers in parallel from the start.

## Outcome

Twelve months after introducing the practice, the organization faced a regulatory change that would have caught them completely off guard eighteen months earlier. Instead, two leaders had flagged early signals of it in Prospective conversations four months before it became official. The organization had already run a small experiment adjusting one of its intake processes and had a clear response ready before the announcement arrived.

The board asked how the leadership team had anticipated it so well. Renata's answer was simple: "We stopped waiting for surprises and started paying attention every month."
