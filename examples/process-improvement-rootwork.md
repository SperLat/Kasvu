# Example: Process Improvement in Practice

> This is an illustrative example based on composite real-world patterns. The characters and organizations are fictional. The situations are not.

## Context

Priya leads an operations team at a healthcare technology company. Her team is responsible for onboarding new clients — getting them from signed contract to fully active on the platform.

The onboarding process had been built three years earlier for clients that were small, technically simple, and largely self-sufficient. The company had since moved upmarket. New clients were larger, had more complex technical environments, and required more coordination across more internal teams. The old process had not changed.

The result was visible everywhere. Average onboarding time had grown from six weeks to fourteen. Client satisfaction during onboarding was the lowest it had ever been. Three clients had churned within sixty days of going live, citing a painful onboarding experience as the primary reason. The team was exhausted.

The instinct was to hire more people. Priya disagreed. More people running a broken process would produce more of the same results, faster.

She recognized this as a Rootwork situation. The process was not sound and in need of refinement. It was no longer fit for purpose and needed to be rebuilt.

## What she did

### Go to the Gemba first

Before proposing any changes, Priya spent two weeks observing the onboarding process as it was actually being run — not as it was documented.

She sat with the team during client calls. She watched how handoffs happened between sales, implementation, and technical support. She asked each team member where they spent time that felt wasteful and where they felt the process failed the client most visibly.

What she found was different from what the documentation described. The formal process had eleven steps. The team was actually running seventeen, because six workarounds had been added over time to handle problems the original process had not anticipated. None of the workarounds were documented. They existed only in the institutional memory of the three most experienced team members.

The process on paper and the process in practice were not the same process.

### Diagnose before rebuilding

Priya spent one week with the team mapping the process as it was actually being run — all seventeen steps, including the workarounds, with the reason each one existed.

This step was slower than she wanted it to be but more important than she had expected. Several of the workarounds existed for good reasons. Removing them without understanding why they had been added would have rebuilt the same problems in new form.

By the end of the week the team had a clear picture of three things: which steps created genuine value for the client, which steps existed only because of internal coordination failures, and which steps were workarounds covering for a gap the original process had never addressed.

### Rebuild around the client experience

Priya rebuilt the onboarding process starting from a single question: what does a client need to feel confident, informed, and unblocked at every stage of this process?

The rebuilt process had eight steps instead of seventeen. It eliminated four internal handoffs that clients never saw but consistently caused delays. It introduced two checkpoints — one at day seven and one at day thirty — where a team member proactively contacted the client to surface problems before they became complaints.

She ran the new process first as an agile pilot with two new clients, measuring both time to activation and client sentiment through a short weekly check-in survey. Both clients onboarded in under seven weeks. Both gave the highest satisfaction scores the team had recorded in two years.

### Roll out and protect the change

After the pilot Priya rolled the new process out to the full team. She did two things to protect the change from reverting.

First, she documented the new process in the same place the team actually worked — not in a separate handbook nobody read, but embedded in the project management tool the team used every day. The process had to be easier to follow than to work around.

Second, she told the team explicitly: if you find yourself working around this process, tell me immediately. A workaround is a signal that the process has a gap. We will fix the process, not accumulate another undocumented step.

## What worked

Going to the Gemba before proposing any changes was the decision that made everything else possible. The process on paper would have led to a rebuild that solved the wrong problems. The process in practice revealed where the real friction was.

The pilot gave the team confidence before the full rollout. Two successful clients made the case for the new process more convincingly than any internal presentation could have.

## What was harder than expected

The diagnosis phase created temporary discomfort. Some team members felt that mapping the workarounds was an implicit criticism of how they had been doing their jobs. Priya had to be deliberate about framing it as an audit of the system, not an evaluation of the people.

The rollout also surfaced a dependency nobody had anticipated: the new process required a faster response time from the technical support team, who had not been part of the rebuild conversation. That gap required an additional two weeks of coordination before the process could run cleanly.

## What Priya would do differently

She would have included technical support in the diagnosis phase from the start. The rebuild had focused on the onboarding team's experience of the process without fully mapping the dependencies on adjacent teams. Involving them earlier would have surfaced the response time issue before the rollout rather than during it.

## Outcome

Six months after the new process launched, average onboarding time had fallen from fourteen weeks to seven. Client satisfaction during onboarding had increased to the highest level in the company's history. No clients had churned during onboarding in the six months since the rebuild.

The team was also less exhausted. Not because the work had become easier, but because they were no longer spending half their energy navigating a process that fought them at every step.

Priya described it this way: "We were not bad at onboarding. We were running a process designed for a company we no longer were. Once we built something that fit who we had become, the team already knew how to do the work."
