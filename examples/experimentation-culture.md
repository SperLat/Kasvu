# Example: Building an Experimentation Culture

> This is an illustrative example based on composite real-world patterns. The characters and organizations are fictional. The situations are not.

## Context

The product team at a mid-sized e-commerce company had a problem they could not name precisely. They shipped consistently, met their deadlines, and had a clean roadmap. But nothing they shipped felt surprising. Every feature was safe, expected, and incremental.

The team lead, Sofia, had been pushing for more innovation for two years. She had gotten nowhere because the word "innovation" triggered a familiar response: a working group, a brainstorm session, a slide deck, and then silence as the roadmap absorbed everything back into the familiar.

The problem was not that the team lacked ideas. It was that the system had no real pathway for an idea to become a test without first becoming a project, which meant it had to compete for roadmap space, which meant it almost never happened.

Sofia found Pillar 4 of Kasvu and recognized the problem immediately. The team did not have innate permission. Every experiment had to become a proposal before it could become a test.

## What she did

### Name the actual problem

Sofia stopped using the word innovation. She started using the word experiment.

She told the team: "We are not going to try to be more innovative. We are going to make it easier to run small tests. That is it."

The reframe mattered. Innovation felt like a character trait the team either had or did not have. Experimentation felt like a practice anyone could participate in.

### Create a lightweight experiment pathway

Sofia introduced a simple rule: any team member could propose an experiment using a single page that answered four questions.

- What problem are we testing a solution to?
- What is the smallest version of this test we can run?
- How will we know if it worked?
- How long will it take?

If the test could be run in under two weeks and required no additional budget, it was approved automatically. No proposal. No committee. No roadmap negotiation.

This was the innate permission principle made structural.

### Run the first cycle visibly

Sofia ran the first experiment herself to demonstrate the cycle. She picked a small, low-risk change to the checkout flow, ran it for ten days, measured both conversion rate and user sentiment through a short post-purchase survey, and shared the results with the team regardless of outcome.

The experiment produced a small improvement. More importantly, it produced a story. The team could see what the cycle looked like from start to finish in real time.

### Protect learning over winning

Two of the first five experiments the team ran did not produce the hoped-for result. Sofia made a point of spending as much time on the debrief for those two as she did for the ones that worked.

She asked the same question after every experiment regardless of outcome: "What do we know now that we did not know before?" That question slowly replaced the implicit question the team had been carrying: "Did we get it right?"

## What worked

Making the approval pathway structural rather than cultural was the most important change. Telling people they had permission to experiment was not enough. They needed a system that made experimentation easier than not experimenting.

The dual measurement practice changed how the team thought about success. Tracking sentiment alongside metrics revealed that two experiments that looked like wins on conversion data were creating friction that showed up in customer support volume two weeks later.

## What was harder than expected

The first month felt slower than Sofia expected. People were cautious about using the new pathway even after it existed. The habit of asking permission before acting was deeply ingrained.

She solved this by asking in team meetings: "Is there anything anyone is thinking about testing?" and then helping the first person who raised an idea move it through the pathway in the meeting itself. Seeing it work in real time for a colleague was more convincing than any explanation.

## What Sofia would do differently

She would connect the experimentation practice to Prospective thinking earlier. Many of the best experiments the team eventually ran came from signals the front-line engineers and designers were seeing but had no formal channel to surface. Building the Prospective dialogue practice alongside the experimentation pathway would have generated better experiments from the start.

## Outcome

In the six months following the introduction of the new pathway, the team ran twenty-three experiments. Eleven produced improvements that were adopted permanently. Eight produced learning that informed later decisions. Four were stopped early when the data was clear.

The roadmap did not change dramatically. What changed was that the space between the roadmap items became productive for the first time.
