# Example: Small Team Rollout

## Context

Maya is an operations manager at a mid-sized logistics company. She leads a team of ten people responsible for coordinating last-mile delivery across three regions. The team is competent and reliable but has been running on autopilot for about eighteen months. People do their jobs, hit their numbers, and go home. There is no real sense of growth, no honest conversation about what could be better, and no shared language for talking about the work beyond the metrics.

Maya discovered Kasvu through a leadership forum and decided to introduce it gradually, without announcement, without a big reveal, and without asking anyone to read a framework document on their lunch break.

## What she did

### Month 1: Culture first

Maya started with Pillar 1 and did not mention Kasvu by name.

She changed one thing: she started every weekly team meeting with a single question that had nothing to do with the numbers. Questions like "what was the hardest part of your week and what made it hard?" or "what is one thing that happened this week that you are genuinely proud of?"

The first two weeks were awkward. People gave short answers and waited to move on to the agenda. By week three, someone gave a real answer. By week five, the check-in was taking fifteen minutes and nobody was trying to cut it short.

She also stopped asking "who caused this?" when something went wrong and started asking "what in our process allowed this to happen?" She said it out loud every time. Within six weeks the team started using the same language with each other.

### Month 2: Introduce the 1:1 framework

In the second month Maya moved her 1:1s from status check-ins to development conversations using the Kasvu 1:1 framework.

She told each person directly: "I want to use our time differently. I am less interested in what you are working on and more interested in how you are growing."

Three people found it uncomfortable at first. One person told her it felt strange to talk about development when there was so much operational pressure. Maya acknowledged the pressure and asked: "What if we treated your development as part of how we handle the pressure, not separate from it?" That reframe shifted the conversation.

By the end of month two, two people on the team had started informally mentoring newer members without being asked. The Echo Effect had started without Maya naming it.

### Month 3: Run the first retrospective opening

In month three Maya ran the retrospective opening framework for the first time before a quarterly team review.

She read the parable aloud. The room was quiet in a way it had not been before. She asked: "Where does the wind blow to for us right now?"

One person said the team had been so focused on efficiency that they had stopped noticing what customers were actually experiencing. Another said they had been sculpting their processes for the last year and calling it improvement.

That conversation led directly to a process change that reduced a recurring customer complaint by forty percent over the following quarter.

## What worked

Starting with Culture and Engagement before introducing any other pillar gave the team the safety it needed to be honest when the harder conversations came.

Introducing changes without labeling them as a framework reduced resistance. People adopted new behaviors before they were asked to adopt a new philosophy.

The parable did more work in one session than three months of operational messaging had done.

## What was harder than expected

The 1:1 shift took longer than Maya expected for some team members. Two people had worked under managers who used 1:1s as performance monitoring tools and needed more time to trust that the conversation was genuinely developmental.

Pillar 6 was the last to take root. Prospective thinking felt abstract to a team that was trained to solve today's problem today. It required repeated and patient investment before people started bringing signals from the field into leadership conversations naturally.

## What Maya would do differently

She would introduce the parable earlier. She waited until month three out of caution, but in hindsight the story would have set the tone for everything else from the start.

She would also name Kasvu sooner. Keeping it unnamed helped with initial adoption but created a moment later where team members felt slightly deceived when they learned there was a philosophy behind what they had been doing. Transparency earlier would have been more consistent with the philosophy itself.

## Outcome

Eight months after Maya began, the team ran its first Garden Audit. The ratings were uneven in places, but the conversation was the most honest the team had ever had. Two people asked if they could introduce Kasvu to other teams in the organization.

The pine was growing.
