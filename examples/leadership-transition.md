# Example: Leadership Transition

> This is an illustrative example based on composite real-world patterns. The characters and organizations are fictional. The situations are not.

## Context

Daniel inherited a customer success team of fourteen people at a software company. His predecessor had been in the role for four years and had built a team that was technically strong, process-heavy, and deeply uncomfortable with ambiguity.

The previous leader had run the team on a sculpting model without knowing it. Every process was documented to the point of rigidity. Every decision escalated upward. People were excellent at following the playbook and lost when the playbook did not cover the situation. Initiative had been quietly trained out of them.

Daniel had three months before the company's busiest season. He did not have time for a slow cultural rebuild, but he also knew that pushing hard in the wrong direction would make things worse.

## What he did

### Week 1 to 2: Listen before anything else

Daniel ran a compressed version of the Garden Audit in his first two weeks, not as a formal session but as a series of individual conversations using the health check questions as a guide.

He asked each person: "What is working well that I should protect? What is getting in your way that I should know about? What would you change if you could?"

What he heard was consistent. People felt safe following instructions and unsafe making judgment calls. They had been told what to do for so long that they had stopped trusting their own instincts. The culture was not toxic; it was frozen.

### Week 3 to 6: Unfreeze permission

Daniel's first intervention was explicit and direct. He told the team in a group meeting: "You have more permission than you think you do. If something is obviously the right thing for the customer, do it and tell me about it afterward. You do not need to ask first."

Then he backed it up. Every time someone acted without asking and got it right, he named it publicly. Every time someone acted without asking and got it wrong, he asked "what would you do differently?" and moved on without blame.

Within four weeks people were making more decisions independently. The escalation rate dropped by half.

### Week 6 to 10: Introduce Experimentation

Once the team had some confidence in their own judgment, Daniel introduced Pillar 4 explicitly.

He told them: "We are going to start running small experiments. Not everything needs to be perfect before we try it. We just need to be clear about what we are testing and what we are trying to learn."

He ran the first experiment himself alongside the team: a small change to the onboarding check-in call format. He used the experimentation cycle visibly, narrating each stage so the team could see how it worked. The experiment produced a modest improvement in customer satisfaction scores and a significant improvement in team confidence.

### Week 10 to 12: First Garden Session before the busy season

Three weeks before the busy season Daniel ran a shortened version of the quarterly Garden Session.

He focused on two questions: "What grew in the last ten weeks?" and "Where does the wind blow to going into our busiest period?"

The team identified three things that had genuinely grown: decision-making confidence, customer empathy in conversations, and willingness to flag problems early. They also identified one significant risk heading into the busy season: a process that worked fine at normal volume but would likely break under peak load.

They fixed it before the season started.

## What worked

The explicit permission conversation was the most important single intervention. The team did not need a new framework; they needed someone to tell them it was safe to think.

Running the health check as individual conversations rather than a group session in the first two weeks gave Daniel accurate information without putting people on the spot before they trusted him.

## What was harder than expected

Development was the slowest pillar to move. People who had been in execution mode for years needed more time to reconnect with the idea that their own growth was part of their job. Daniel underestimated how much the previous culture had separated "doing the work" from "developing as a person."

## What Daniel would do differently

He would have started development conversations earlier. He introduced the 1:1 framework in week six, but looking back the team needed someone investing in their growth from week one, not just unfreezing their permission to act. Unfreezing permission and investing in development could have run in parallel from the start. The team would have moved faster on both.

## Outcome

The team made it through the busy season with fewer escalations, higher customer satisfaction scores, and lower burnout than the previous year. Three people who had never shown leadership ambition before started asking about growth paths.

Daniel described it this way: "I did not build a new team. I just stopped blocking the one that was already there."
