# Example: The Echo Effect in Practice

> This is an illustrative example based on composite real-world patterns. The characters and organizations are fictional. The situations are not.

## Context

Marcus leads an engineering team of twelve at a mid-sized fintech company. The team is technically strong but development has always been informal — something that happened by accident when a senior engineer happened to take an interest in someone junior, and did not happen at all when they did not.

Two things were true at the same time. The three most senior engineers on the team were stretched thin and quietly burning out. The four most junior engineers were growing slowly and waiting to be told what to do. The connection between those two facts had not been named.

Marcus found Pillar 2 of Kasvu and recognized the pattern immediately. Development was concentrated in a few people instead of moving through the system. The seniors were carrying what the system should have been distributing.

## What he did

### Name the pattern honestly

Marcus started with individual 1:1s using the Kasvu 1:1 framework. He asked each senior engineer two questions: what are you spending your time on that someone else on this team could be doing with the right support, and is there someone on the team you could be developing right now?

Every senior engineer had an answer to the first question. None of them had thought seriously about the second.

He asked each junior engineer: what do you want to get better at, and who on this team do you think could help you get there?

Every junior engineer had an answer to both.

The information existed. Nobody had connected it.

### Make the pairing explicit

Marcus made four deliberate pairings — one senior engineer with one junior engineer each — based on what the junior wanted to develop and what the senior was already doing that required those skills.

He told each senior engineer directly: your job is not to do this work for them. Your job is to bring them into the work with you, explain your thinking, and give them increasing responsibility as their judgment develops. Start small. Let them make mistakes that are safe to make.

He told each junior engineer: your job is not to wait for instruction. Your job is to show up curious, ask questions, try things, and tell your mentor what you are noticing.

He gave each pair one shared piece of real work — not a training exercise, not a side project, but something that actually mattered to the team — as the vehicle for the development relationship.

### Close the loop with the Echo Effect question

In every subsequent 1:1 Marcus asked the same two questions he had asked at the start: what are you learning right now, and who are you helping learn?

The second question was the one that changed behavior. People who had never thought of themselves as developers of others started paying attention differently. One junior engineer, three months into her pairing, started informally answering questions from the newest team member without being asked. She had not been told to. She had simply internalized that passing it forward was part of how the team worked.

That was the Echo Effect beginning.

### Protect the time

The most persistent threat to the pairing practice was operational pressure. When the team was under deadline, development conversations were the first thing to disappear.

Marcus made one rule: 1:1s between pairs were protected time. They could be shortened but not cancelled. A thirty-minute conversation under pressure was worth more than a perfect hour that never happened.

He also watched for the pattern where seniors reverted to doing the work themselves instead of developing others through it. When he saw it, he named it directly — not as criticism, but as a reminder that efficiency now at the cost of development later was a trade the team could not afford to keep making.

## What worked

Making the pairings explicit and assigning real work as the vehicle was the most important structural decision. Informal mentorship happens by accident. Explicit pairing with real stakes happens by design.

The Echo Effect question — is there someone you are helping learn right now — changed how people saw their own role. Engineers who had never considered themselves mentors started acting like them because someone had directly asked them to.

## What was harder than expected

Two of the four pairings were uneven at first. One senior engineer struggled to let the junior make mistakes without jumping in. One junior engineer was passive and needed more direct prompting than Marcus had anticipated.

Marcus handled both by coaching the senior on what it means to develop rather than rescue, and by being more explicit with the junior about what initiative looked like in practice. Neither issue resolved immediately, but both improved over six to eight weeks.

## What Marcus would do differently

He would have introduced the development pairings at the same time as the 1:1 framework, not two months later. He had started the Kasvu 1:1s first, let them run for two months, and then added the pairings. The 1:1s were useful on their own, but the development relationships were what actually moved the system. He would run them in parallel from the start.

## Outcome

Eight months after the pairings began, Marcus ran a Garden Audit. The two metrics that had changed most were development and culture. Three of the four junior engineers had taken on responsibilities they had not held before. Two of the three senior engineers said they felt less stretched, not because their workload had decreased but because they were no longer the only ones who could handle certain problems.

One of the junior engineers, in her Garden Audit response, wrote: I finally feel like I am growing on purpose instead of by accident.

The Echo Effect was working. Growth was moving through the system instead of concentrating at the top.
