# Example: A Root Reading in Practice

> This is an illustrative example based on composite real-world patterns. The characters and organizations are fictional. The situations are not.

## Context

Lena leads a customer support team of eighteen people at a fast-growing software company. Over a three-month period, the team's customer satisfaction scores had been quietly declining. Not dramatically — nothing that triggered an alarm — but consistently. Every month slightly worse than the month before.

The instinct was to treat it as a volume problem. The team was handling forty percent more tickets than the previous year. More tickets, more pressure, more mistakes. The proposed solution was to hire two more people.

Lena pushed back. She had seen volume increases handled well before. Something else was happening. She called a Root Reading.

## What she did

### Name what happened

Lena started the session by establishing a shared factual account. She asked the team to set aside their interpretations and reconstruct the sequence of events as observable facts.

What emerged was this: satisfaction scores had started declining three months ago, two weeks after the company had launched a new product feature. Ticket volume had increased, but so had a specific type of ticket — customers confused about how the new feature interacted with existing workflows they relied on.

The team had been resolving these tickets correctly. But they had been resolving them slowly, because the answers required checking with the product team, who were hard to reach during the post-launch period. Average resolution time on that ticket type was four times higher than the team's normal rate.

Nobody had named this pattern explicitly before the session. Everyone had felt it. Nobody had connected the dots.

### Follow the roots

With the sequence clear, Lena asked the team to trace why at each stage.

Why were resolution times high? Because the support team did not have documented answers for the new feature's edge cases.

Why did they not have documented answers? Because the product team had not created support documentation before launch.

Why had support documentation not been created before launch? Because there was no formal handoff process between product and support before a new feature went live. The support team learned about new features when customers started asking about them.

That was the root. Not volume. Not individual performance. A missing process at the seam between two teams.

### Read the soil

Lena asked what this failure revealed about the conditions that produced it.

The team identified two things. First, support was not included in the product launch process at all — not in planning, not in testing, not in documentation. They were downstream of every decision but responsible for the customer experience those decisions created.

Second, the team had noticed the pattern forming but had not escalated it because they were not sure it was their place to say something about a product process. The culture had not made it clear that surfacing cross-functional problems was part of their role.

The information existed. The channel and the permission did not.

### Name what must change

The structural change the session produced was specific: no product feature would go live without a support readiness review — a short meeting between product and support before launch to document edge cases, anticipated questions, and escalation paths.

Lena owned the process design. The head of product agreed to make it a launch requirement. A target date was set for the first review to be run before the next scheduled release.

The session also produced a secondary commitment: Lena would tell her team explicitly that flagging cross-functional gaps was not overstepping. It was part of the job.

### Close with what held

Before ending the session, Lena asked the team to name what had not broken.

Two things came up. First, individual resolution quality had remained high throughout — the team was giving correct answers, just slowly. The standard of care had held even under pressure. Second, one team member had started keeping an informal log of the confusing tickets three weeks earlier, which gave the Root Reading its clearest evidence. That instinct to document was worth naming and protecting.

## What worked

Staying in the sequence of facts before asking why prevented the session from becoming a blame conversation about individual performance. The root — a missing cross-functional process — only became visible because the group resisted jumping to conclusions in the first twenty minutes.

The closing step mattered more than Lena expected. Naming what held changed the tone of what came after. The team left the session feeling capable, not criticized.

## What was harder than expected

The product team's initial response to the proposed support readiness review was defensive. They heard it as criticism of their launch process. Lena had to be deliberate about framing the change as a structural fix rather than a verdict on anyone's judgment. The Root Reading's no-blame principle had to extend beyond the session itself into the conversation with stakeholders who were not in the room.

## What Lena would do differently

She would have run a Root Reading earlier — not after three months of declining scores, but after the first month when the pattern was still small. A Root Reading does not require a major failure. It requires a signal that something systemic is present. Waiting for the signal to become loud costs time and customer trust that does not need to be lost.

## Outcome

The support readiness review became a permanent part of the product launch process. Over the following quarter, average resolution time on new feature tickets dropped to within normal range. Satisfaction scores recovered within two months.

More importantly, the support team started flagging cross-functional issues they had been absorbing silently for years. Three additional process gaps were identified and fixed in the six months that followed — none of which had triggered a formal complaint, but all of which had been creating quiet friction the system had been paying for without knowing it.

The root had been diseased for longer than anyone realized. The reading made it visible.
