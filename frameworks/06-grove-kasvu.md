# The Grove Framework: Kasvu Prospective Practice

> 📋 Ready to use this? See the companion template: [06-grove-template.md](../templates/06-grove-template.md)

This framework guides a recurring 15-30 minute dialogue practice that builds anticipation into the rhythm of a team or organization. The Grove is not a planning session. It is a listening habit — structured enough to be consistent, light enough to happen every month without resistance.

## Purpose

Most organizations are not surprised by change because they lacked information. They are surprised because they had no practice for taking signals seriously before those signals became crises. The Grove exists to close that gap.

The name comes from the same forest the philosophy lives in. A grove is not a single tree. It is a collection of trees with different heights, different vantage points, and different exposure to the wind. What one tree cannot see, another can. The Grove meeting works the same way — different people, different altitudes, the same ground.

## When to use it

Run The Grove when:
- You want to build Prospective thinking into the regular rhythm of a team or leadership group.
- Your organization tends to react to change rather than anticipate it.
- You sense that useful signals exist inside the organization but have no formal channel to surface.
- You are running a quarterly Garden Session and want richer input on the environment before it.

The Grove works at any level — a team of five, a leadership group of twelve, or a cross-functional signal-sharing practice between layers.

## What you need

- 15-30 minutes at the end of a regular meeting you are already running. Do not create a new meeting for this.
- Each participant having paid attention during the month. No slides. No preparation beyond their own observations.
- A shared document where signals are captured and visible after the session.

## The conversation flow

### Open with the question (2 minutes)

The facilitator opens with one of three questions. Rotate them across sessions so the practice stays fresh and covers different types of signals:

- What are we seeing more of that we were not seeing six months ago?
- What is one thing a customer, team member, or person outside this room said recently that stayed with you?
- What is one thing changing in our environment that we have not yet named as a team?

Each person answers. No discussion yet. Just listening.

### Surface the patterns (10-15 minutes)

Once everyone has shared, the facilitator asks:
- Did anything overlap or connect across what people said?
- Is there a signal here that has appeared more than once across the last two or three sessions?
- Is there anything we have been noticing individually but not saying out loud as a group?

Signals that appear consistently across two or more sessions are worth naming explicitly. They become candidates for a small experiment or a deeper conversation in the next Garden Session.

### Name what needs to go somewhere (5 minutes)

Not every signal requires action. But some need to be placed somewhere — into the experimentation pathway, onto the agenda for the next strategy session, or assigned to one person to watch and report back on.

Ask:
- Is there anything here that needs to move into an experiment?
- Is there anything here that leadership needs to hear if they are not in this room?
- Is there anything here that we have named three times now and still have not done anything about?

That last question is the most important one. A signal that keeps appearing without response is not a prospective signal anymore. It is a failure waiting to happen.

### Close (2 minutes)

Capture everything in the shared document. Note which signals are new, which are recurring, and which have been assigned somewhere. End the session.

## Facilitator notes

The quality of a Grove session depends almost entirely on the quality of what people bring into it. Vague generalities produce vague conversations. Sharp, specific observations produce sharp conversations.

If the first few sessions are uneven, share an example of a strong signal versus a weak one at the start of the next session. A strong signal is specific, grounded in something observed, and points toward something changing. A weak signal is abstract, secondhand, or simply restates something everyone already knows.

The Grove is most valuable when it includes people from different altitudes — those closest to the customer or the work, and those with enough distance to see patterns across the whole. If your Grove only includes one level of the organization, you are missing signals.

## How The Grove connects to other practices

The Grove feeds the quarterly Garden Session with richer environmental input than a leadership team can generate on its own. Signals that appear consistently in Grove sessions become the raw material for the Garden Session's environmental review.

The Grove also feeds the experimentation pathway directly. A signal that keeps appearing is a hypothesis waiting to be tested. The step from "we keep seeing this" to "let's run a small experiment" should be short.

## Suggested time

| Part | Time |
|---|---|
| Open with the question | 2 minutes |
| Surface the patterns | 10-15 minutes |
| Name what needs to go somewhere | 5 minutes |
| Close and capture | 2 minutes |
| **Total** | **19-24 minutes** |
