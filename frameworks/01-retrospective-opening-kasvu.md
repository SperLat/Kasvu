# Retrospective Opening: The Kasvu Method

> 📋 Ready to use this? See the companion template: [01-retro-template.md](../templates/01-retro-template.md)

## Purpose

This framework helps a leader open a team retrospective in a way that sets the right tone before any agenda item is discussed.

Most retrospectives fail not because of bad questions but because the room is not ready. People arrive carrying the noise of the week. They are in execution mode, not reflection mode. This opening exists to make the shift.
 
It uses the Kasvu parable as the entry point: a short story that asks the team to step back from the day-to-day and look at how they are actually working together.

## When to use it

Use this opening when:

- The team needs to slow down before it can think clearly.
- There is tension in the room that has not been named yet.
- The retrospective covers something significant: a difficult quarter, a major failure, a transition, or a moment of real growth.
- You want the team to reflect not just on what happened, but on how they are leading themselves.

It works less well for purely operational retrospectives where speed matters more than depth. Read the room before deciding.

## What you need

- The parable text from `docs/parable.md`
- A quiet space with minimal interruption 
- 15 to 30 minutes before the main retrospective agenda begins
- No slides required

## The opening flow

### Step 1: Set the intention (2 minutes)

Before reading anything, tell the team why you are starting this way.

Keep it short. Something like:

"Before we get into the agenda, I want to spend a few minutes outside of it.
I am going to read something short. I just ask that you listen without trying to solve anything yet."

Do not over-explain. The less you frame it, the more space the story has to land.

### Step 2: Read the parable (5 to 7 minutes)

Read the parable aloud from beginning to end.

A few notes on how to read it:

- Read slowly. This is not a briefing.
- Pause briefly after each section heading.
- When you reach the Master's closing words, let the silence sit for a moment   before continuing.
- Do not editorialize while reading. Let the story carry itself.

If reading aloud feels unnatural for your context, you can ask the team to read it silently and give them 5 minutes to do so. The effect is different but still
useful.

### Step 3: Let it breathe (1 to 2 minutes)

After the last line, do not speak immediately.

Give the room 60 to 90 seconds of silence. This is not dead time. It is the moment where the story moves from heard to felt. Resist the urge to fill it.

### Step 4: Open with one question (10 to 15 minutes)

Ask only one question to start. Do not stack questions.

Choose the one that fits the moment:

**If the team has been moving too fast:**
> "Are we building a garden or shaping a statue right now?"

**If the team has been avoiding a hard conversation:**
> "What is the pine in our team that we have been ignoring?"

**If the team has had a significant win:**
> "What did we cultivate to make that possible?"

**If the team has had a significant failure:**
> "What did the system not give this team that it needed to grow?"

**If the context is more open:**
> "Where does the wind blow to for us right now?"

Let people respond in their own time. Do not rush to the next question. One good question held long enough is worth more than five questions covered quickly.

### Step 5: Transition into the agenda

When the conversation has found its natural pause, close the opening simply.

Something like:

"This is the lens I want us to carry into today's session. Let's get into it."

Then move into your retrospective agenda without ceremony.

## Facilitator notes

The parable works because it is indirect. It gives people permission to talk about real things without feeling exposed. When someone says "I think we have been sculpting lately," they are saying something honest about how the team is operating without having to point fingers.

Your job during the reflection is to listen more than you speak. Ask follow-up questions only when the conversation stalls. Be socratic. The most useful follow-up is usually the simplest: "Can you say more about that?"

Avoid summarizing too early. Let the team hold the ambiguity a little longer than feels comfortable. That is where the real reflection happens.

## Pillars activated

This framework directly engages:

- Pillar 1: Culture and Engagement — by creating psychological safety before the agenda begins.
- Pillar 4: Experimentation — by treating the retrospective itself as a learning space rather than a reporting exercise.
- Pillar 6: Prospective — by inviting the team to look at where things are heading, not only where they have been.

## Suggested time

| Part | Time |
|---|---|
| Set the intention | 2 minutes |
| Read the parable | 5 to 7 minutes |
| Silence | 1 to 2 minutes |
| Reflection and discussion | 10 to 15 minutes |
| Transition | 1 minute |
| **Total** | **19 to 27 minutes** |
