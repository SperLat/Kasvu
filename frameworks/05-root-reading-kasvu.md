# Root Reading Framework: The Kasvu Method

> 📋 Ready to use this? See the companion template: [05-root-reading-template.md](../templates/05-root-reading-template.md)

This framework guides a 60-90 minute session to examine a significant failure or near-miss — not to assign blame, but to uncover the systemic conditions that made it possible. The purpose is not to fix the symptom. It is to read the soil that produced it.

## Purpose

A Root Reading is Kasvu's disciplined response to failure. When a branch breaks, the gardener does not examine only what broke. They trace it back to the roots — what the soil produced, what conditions were present long before the visible failure appeared.

This is not a performance review. It is not a blame session. It is collective inquiry into what the system allowed to happen and why. The session ends with one structural change that addresses the root, not the branch.

## When to use it

Convene a Root Reading when:
- A significant failure reached the customer in a way that damaged trust.
- A process produced the wrong outcome repeatedly despite previous fixes.
- Something unexpected happened that the system should have anticipated but did not.
- A near-miss revealed a structural fragility worth understanding before it becomes a real failure.

Small, isolated errors are handled through normal feedback and correction. A Root Reading is reserved for failures that reveal something meaningful about the health of the system itself.

## What you need

- 60-90 minutes of uninterrupted time.
- 4-8 participants from multiple layers: those closest to the failure, those responsible for the process, and at least one person with enough distance to ask questions without existing assumptions.
- A shared surface — document or whiteboard — for capturing the sequence of events.
- The discipline to stay in inquiry longer than feels comfortable before moving to action.

## The conversation flow

### Step 1: Name what happened (10 minutes)

Establish a shared, factual account of what occurred. Not why. Not who. Just what happened, in sequence, as clearly as possible.

The facilitator's job is to keep the group in observable facts until the picture is complete. Ask:
- What was the first thing that happened?
- What happened next?
- What was the customer impact?

People will arrive with interpretations already formed. Hold the group in facts until the full sequence is visible to everyone in the room.

### Step 2: Follow the roots (20 minutes)

Once the sequence is clear, begin asking why at each stage. Keep asking until you reach something structural — a condition in the system, a missing resource, an unclear process, an unspoken assumption, or a cultural dynamic that made the failure possible.

The orienting question is: what did the system produce, and what in the system made that outcome possible?

Do not stop at individual actions. Each action has a condition behind it. Trace each one back until you reach soil.

### Step 3: Read the soil (20 minutes)

This is the step most post-mortems skip. Beyond the immediate failure, ask what the roots reveal about the health of the ground itself.

A single fallen branch may be an isolated event. Roots that are shallow, tangled, or starved are a systemic condition.

Ask:
- What does this failure tell us about the culture that produced it?
- What did this team or system not have that it needed?
- What assumption were we collectively making that this failure has now challenged?
- What would have had to be different for this outcome to have been unlikely rather than inevitable?

### Step 4: Name what must change (15 minutes)

A Root Reading is not complete until it produces at least one structural change — not a behavioral reminder or a new rule, but a change to the conditions that made the failure possible.

The change must address the root, not the symptom. A process patch that prevents this specific failure while leaving the underlying condition intact is not a solution. It is a pruning that leaves the root diseased.

Ask:
- What is the one structural change that would make this outcome less likely?
- Who owns it?
- How will we know it is working?

### Step 5: Close with what held (5-10 minutes)

End every Root Reading by naming what did not break. This is not optimism for its own sake. In most significant failures, something in the system held — a person who caught something early, a process that contained the damage, a relationship that allowed honest conversation to happen quickly.

Naming what held tells the team what is worth protecting as they rebuild.

## Facilitator notes

The ratio that matters most in a Root Reading is how long the group stays in inquiry before moving to solutions. Most groups want to jump to fixes within the first ten minutes. The facilitator's job is to hold them in the question longer. The most important thing said in a Root Reading is usually the hardest thing to say — and it almost never comes early.

If the session ends without a structural change, it has not finished its work. A Root Reading that produces only a list of observations is a better conversation than usual but not a Root Reading.

## Principles

- No blame. The root, not the person, is what is being examined.
- No premature solutions. Stay in inquiry longer than feels comfortable.
- No incomplete closings. A Root Reading without a structural change is unfinished.
- Honesty over comfort. The most important insight is usually the hardest to say.
- What broke and what held both deserve equal attention.

## Pillars activated

- Pillar 1: Culture and Engagement — psychological safety, no-blame inquiry.
- Pillar 3: Process Improvement — surfacing systemic friction and structural fixes.
- Pillar 5: Quality — the framework lives inside this pillar; see [05-quality.md](../docs/pillars/05-quality.md).
- Pillar 6: Prospective — asking what the failure reveals about what the system failed to anticipate.

## Suggested time

| Part | Time |
|---|---|
| Name what happened | 10 minutes |
| Follow the roots | 20 minutes |
| Read the soil | 20 minutes |
| Name what must change | 15 minutes |
| Close with what held | 5-10 minutes |
| **Total** | **70-75 minutes** |
