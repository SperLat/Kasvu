# 1:1 Framework: The Kasvu Method

> 📋 Ready to use this? See the companion template: [02-1on1-template.md](../templates/02-1on1-template.md)

## Purpose

This framework helps a leader run a 1:1 conversation that develops the person, not just manages the task.

Most 1:1s become status updates. The leader asks what is happening, the person reports, and both leave without anything having shifted. This framework uses the Kasvu pillars as a lens to turn a regular 1:1 into a genuine development conversation.

## When to use it

Use this framework when:

- You want to move beyond status and into the person's actual growth.
- Someone on your team is stuck, plateauing, or disengaged.
- You are onboarding someone and want to establish a development-first tone early.
- You sense something is off but do not know what.
- You want to reinforce the Echo Effect: developing this person so they can develop others.

It works less well as a rigid script. Treat it as a compass, not a checklist.

## What you need

- 30 to 60 minutes of uninterrupted time.
- A genuine intention to listen more than you speak.
- Notes from the previous 1:1 if available.

## The conversation flow

### Step 1: Open with presence (2 to 3 minutes)

Before anything else, check in as a human being.

Ask something simple and mean it:

- "How are you actually doing?"
- "What has the week felt like?"
- "What is taking up the most space in your head right now?"

Do not rush past this. The quality of everything that follows depends on whether the person feels the room is safe enough to be honest.

### Step 2: Reflect on the recent past (5 to 10 minutes)

Ask the person to look back at the period since your last conversation.

Useful questions:

- "What went well that you are proud of?"
- "What did not go the way you wanted?"
- "Where did you feel most like yourself at work?"
- "Where did you feel most out of your depth?"

Listen for what they choose to mention first. That is usually where the real conversation is.

### Step 3: Go deeper on one thing (10 to 15 minutes)

Pick one thread from Step 2 and follow it.

Do not try to cover everything. One thing explored well is worth more than five things mentioned.

If they mentioned a win, explore what made it possible and how to repeat it. If they mentioned a struggle, explore what the system did or did not give them, not what they did wrong.

Questions that go deeper:

- "What do you think made that work?"
- "If you could do that again, what would you change?"
- "What would have helped that you did not have?"
- "What did you learn about yourself in that moment?"

### Step 4: Look at development (10 minutes)

This is the core of the Kasvu 1:1.

Ask questions that connect the recent past to the person's growth trajectory:

- "What skill or judgment do you feel yourself building right now?"
- "What is the next thing you want to get better at?"
- "Where do you feel like you are being stretched in a good way?"
- "Where do you feel like you are not being stretched enough?"
- "Is there someone you are learning from right now? Is there someone you could be teaching?"

The last question is the Echo Effect question. It matters.

### Step 5: Remove friction (5 minutes)

Ask directly what the system is getting wrong for this person.

- "What is slowing you down that I could help remove?"
- "What process or situation keeps creating the same problem for you?"
- "Is there anything you need from me that you have not asked for?"

This is where Process Improvement and Culture meet in a single conversation. The leader's job here is to listen and act, not to explain or defend.

### Step 6: Close with one commitment (2 to 3 minutes)

End every 1:1 with one clear commitment from each side.

Not a list. One thing.

The person commits to something specific they will do or try before the next conversation. You commit to something specific you will do or provide.

Write both down. Start the next 1:1 by returning to them.

## Facilitator notes

The ratio that matters most in a Kasvu 1:1 is how much the other person speaks versus how much you speak. If you are speaking more than a third of the time, you are running a briefing, not a development conversation.

Silence is useful. When someone pauses after a hard question, resist filling the space. The answer they give after sitting with it is almost always better than the first thing they would have said.

If the conversation goes somewhere unexpected, follow it. The framework is a starting shape, not a constraint.

## Pillars activated

- Pillar 1: Culture and Engagement — psychological safety, trust, honest dialogue.
- Pillar 2: Development — the entire framework is a development instrument.
- Pillar 3: Process Improvement — surfacing friction the system creates.
- Pillar 6: Prospective — looking at where the person is heading, not only where they have been.

## Suggested time

| Part | Time |
|---|---|
| Open with presence | 2 to 3 minutes |
| Reflect on the recent past | 5 to 10 minutes |
| Go deeper on one thing | 10 to 15 minutes |
| Look at development | 10 minutes |
| Remove friction | 5 minutes |
| Close with one commitment | 2 to 3 minutes |
| **Total** | **34 to 46 minutes** |
