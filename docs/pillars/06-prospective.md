# Pillar 6: Prospective

## Philosophy

The future should be treated as a daily habit, not an occasional exercise.

Kasvu uses the term Prospective to describe the practice of continuously reading signals, anticipating shifts, and preparing the system before change becomes urgent. It is not prediction for its own sake. It is disciplined attentiveness to where reality is moving.

Prospective is oriented by one constant question:

"Where does the wind blow to?"

In practice this means two things: understanding where things stand right now, and reading where they are heading next.

## Why it matters

Organizations often fail not because they lack effort, but because they respond too late.

When teams are consumed only by the present, they become excellent at handling current demand while becoming blind to emerging conditions. This creates strategic lag.

Kasvu treats future awareness as an everyday responsibility. The goal is not to guess perfectly. The goal is to reduce surprise and increase readiness.

## Core principles

- The future is shaped first by signals, not by headlines.
- Front-line insight is as important as executive perspective.
- Foresight must inform action, not remain abstract.
- Good Prospective work sharpens the quality of present decisions.
- Anticipation is a habit that must be practiced continuously.

## Daily practice

Prospective is built through regular calibration between leadership and front-line teams.

Leaders often have strategic altitude. Front-line teams often have sensory proximity to reality. Kasvu values the dialogue between those two perspectives.

This means asking questions such as:
- What are we seeing more often?
- What feels different than six months ago?
- What new customer expectations are emerging?
- What risks are becoming more likely?
- What opportunities are not yet obvious to everyone?

The purpose is to create a living conversation with the future.

These recurrent meetings function as open spaces where leadership and front-line teams listen to each other without agenda hierarchy. Both parties bring their perspective, share awareness, and distribute responsibility for what they see. This shared visibility strengthens psychological safety by ensuring that no single layer carries the weight of knowing alone.

Depending on context these are called mentorship meetings or open door times when informal and unrestricted, and The Grove -a meeting that is part of a formal mentorship program- 

### The Grove

Prospective is built through a recurring practice Kasvu calls The Grove.

The name comes from a simple observation: in a grove, different trees share the same ground. Older trees do not instruct newer ones. They shelter them while they learn to read the wind and lean with it rather than fight against it.

The Grove is a gathering where leadership and front-line teams come together not to report or decide, but to read the environment collectively. Leaders bring strategic altitude. Front-line teams bring sensory proximity to reality. Neither perspective is complete without the other.

This shared reading strengthens psychological safety by distributing awareness and responsibility across layers. No single level carries the weight of knowing alone.

The questions that orient a Grove conversation are:

- What are we seeing more often?
- What feels different than six months ago?
- What new customer expectations are emerging?
- What risks are becoming more likely?
- What opportunities are not yet obvious to everyone?
- Where does the wind blow to?

The Grove takes two forms depending on context:

**Open Grove** — an informal, unrestricted space where any member of the team can bring what they are seeing without agenda or hierarchy. Run as often as the team needs it.

**Structured Grove** — a formal practice within a mentorship program. Held on a regular cadence with intentional facilitation and continuity across sessions.

Both forms carry the same purpose.

The Grove thinks of time as one, relying on past experiences to teach how to lean into the breeze. You look at your roots and how they have attached to the soil of your learnings.

Both forms carry the same purpose: to grow stronger and with purpose.


## Answer the unanswered question

Prospective is not only about seeing what is coming. It is about noticing what has not yet been addressed.

Kasvu encourages leaders and teams to ask what question will matter next, not only what question matters now. This makes Prospective a bridge between awareness and initiative.

## How this pillar connects

Prospective guides Experimentation by identifying what should be tested before pressure becomes urgent.

It informs Development by revealing what capabilities the system will need next. It improves Process Improvement by helping redesign systems for future conditions rather than only current pain. It sharpens Quality by anticipating customer expectations before they harden into dissatisfaction. It deepens Culture and Engagement by inviting people into a more meaningful relationship with change.

Without Prospective, organizations drift into reaction. With it, they move with greater awareness and intention.
