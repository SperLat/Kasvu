# Pillar 5: Quality

## Philosophy

Quality is defined from the customer's perspective.

A process may look efficient internally and a team may feel satisfied with its output, but if the customer experiences confusion, friction, or disappointment, quality has not been achieved.

In Kasvu, quality is not ornamental. It is a serious expression of care, discipline, and respect towards the customer journey across all layers.

## Why it matters

Every interaction shapes trust.

A single bad experience can outweigh many good ones because customers do not experience quality as an average. They experience it in moments: a delayed response, an unclear answer, a broken promise, a preventable error, an indifferent handoff.

Kasvu treats every bad experience as an unacceptable customer experience. Quality is not only about preventing defects. It is about protecting trust from day one.

## Core principles

- Quality is measured by the customer, not by internal convenience.
- Excellence must be visible in execution, not only in intention.
- Preventing failure is better than recovering from it.
- Errors should be studied without blame and resolved at the root.
- Quality is everyone's responsibility, even when some roles are specialized in raising the bar.

## Ownership model

Kasvu uses a dual model of responsibility for quality.

Teams own the quality of their work. Quality cannot be outsourced to a separate department while the rest of the organization focuses only on speed or output.

A dedicated quality function reinforces this by setting standards, auditing patterns, challenging complacency, and raising the level of discipline across the system.

This creates balance: quality remains embedded in the work while also being reinforced structurally.

## Failure management

When quality breaks, the response must be thoughtful and disciplined.

Kasvu favors post-mortem analysis that examines what happened, why it happened, and what conditions allowed it to happen. The purpose is not punishment. The purpose is learning, correction, and prevention.

A useful review should answer:

- What happened?
- Why did it happen?
- Where did the process fail?
- What was the customer impact?
- What must change so this does not happen again?

Fixes are not considered complete until their effectiveness has been validated with the end user or through clear evidence in the real environment.

## The Root Reading

Before quality breaks in a significant way, Kasvu convenes a Root Reading.

The name comes from the same grove the philosophy lives in. When a tree falls or a branch breaks, the gardener does not look only at what broke. They look at the roots: what the soil produced, what the system allowed, what was present long before the visible failure appeared.

A Root Reading is not a blame session. It is not a performance review. It is a structured act of collective inquiry into what the system produced and why. It is therefore a learning session of the endurances of the forest surrounding the grove more than the grove itself. This means analysis may be external and is encouraged to be, because if you only know what you know and do not seek knowledge beyond your horizon, you will never truly grow.

### When to convene a Root Reading

Convene a Root Reading when:

- A significant failure reached the customer in a way that damaged trust.
- A process produced the wrong outcome repeatedly despite previous attempts to fix it.
- Something unexpected happened that the system should have anticipated but did not.
- A near-miss occurred that revealed a deeper structural fragility worth understanding before it becomes a real failure.

Not every mistake requires a Root Reading. Small, isolated errors are handled through normal feedback and correction. A Root Reading is reserved for failures that reveal something meaningful about the health of the system itself.

### Who participates

A Root Reading works best when it includes people from multiple layers of the system: those closest to where the failure occurred, those responsible for the process that produced it, and at least one person with enough distance to ask questions without assumptions.

This mirrors the structure of The Grove: different trees, different vantage points, the same ground.

### The Root Reading process

**Step 1: Name what happened without judgment**

Begin by establishing a shared, factual account of what occurred. Not why. Not who. Just what happened, in sequence, as clearly as possible.

This step is harder than it sounds. People arrive with interpretations already formed. The facilitator's job is to keep the group in the territory of observable facts until the picture is complete.

**Step 2: Follow the roots**

Once the sequence is clear, begin asking why at each stage.

Kasvu uses a layered inquiry rather than a fixed number of why questions. Keep asking why until you reach something structural: a condition in the system, a missing resource, an unclear process, an unspoken assumption, or a cultural dynamic that made the failure possible.

The question that orients this step is: what did the system produce, and what in the system made that outcome possible?

**Step 3: Read the soil**

This is the step most post-mortems skip.

Beyond the immediate failure, ask what the roots reveal about the health of the ground itself. A single fallen branch may be an isolated event. Roots that are shallow, tangled, or starved of what they need are a systemic condition.

Ask:
- What does this failure tell us about the culture that produced it?
- What did this team or system not have that it needed?
- What assumption were we collectively making that this failure has now challenged?
- What would have had to be different for this outcome to have been unlikely rather than inevitable?

**Step 4: Name what must change**

A Root Reading is not complete until it produces at least one structural change, not a behavioral reminder or a new rule, but a change to the conditions that made the failure possible.

The change should address the root, not the symptom. A process patch that prevents this specific failure from recurring while leaving the underlying condition intact is not a solution. It is a pruning that leaves the root diseased.

**Step 5: Close with what held**

End every Root Reading by naming what did not break.

This is not optimism for its own sake. In most significant failures, something in the system held: a person who caught something early, a process that contained the damage, a relationship that allowed honest conversation to happen quickly. Naming what held tells the team what is worth protecting as they rebuild.

### The difference between a Root Reading and a standard post-mortem

A standard post-mortem asks: what happened, why did it happen, and how do we prevent it from happening again?

A Root Reading asks all of those questions and one more: what does this failure tell us about the health of the system that produced it, and what must we cultivate differently going forward?

The first set of questions fixes the problem. The last question tends the garden.

### Principles of a good Root Reading

- No blame. The root, not the person, is what is being examined.
- No premature solutions. Stay in inquiry longer than feels comfortable before moving to action.
- No incomplete closings. A Root Reading that ends without a structural change has not finished its work.
- Honesty over comfort. The most important thing said in a Root Reading is usually the hardest thing to say.
- What broke and what held both deserve equal attention.


## How this pillar connects

Quality strengthens Culture and Engagement because people are more proud of work that truly serves others.

It depends on Development because stronger judgment and stronger skills produce better outcomes. It benefits from Process Improvement because many quality failures are systemic rather than individual. It is sharpened by Experimentation when tests are measured honestly and standards are not lowered in the name of speed. It is guided by Prospective thinking because anticipating future expectations helps quality evolve before customers are disappointed.

Without Quality, trust erodes. With it, value becomes tangible.
