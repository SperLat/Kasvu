# Pillar 3: Process Improvement

## Philosophy

Process Improvement exists to save internal time and reinvest it in the customer experience.

The purpose of improving a process is to remove friction, reduce waste, increase clarity, and create more capacity for meaningful value. A strong process helps people do excellent work with less confusion, less rework, and less unnecessary effort.

## Why it matters

When internal systems are inefficient, the customer eventually pays for it.

The cost appears as delay, inconsistency, lower quality, frustration, burnout, or missed opportunity. Process Improvement is therefore not only an operational concern; it is a leadership responsibility.

## Core principles

- Save time internally to create more value externally.
- Design with scale in mind from the beginning. Be Scale-aware, not scale-obsessed.
- A garden is designed to grow, not to be fully grown from the start. Design with scale in mind but plant what the season calls for.
- Remove unnecessary friction before adding more control.
- Treat process improvement as a continuous capability, not a one-time project.
- Make problems visible before trying to solve them.

## Improvement methods

Kasvu uses an adaptive approach to improvement rather than a single prescribed doctrine.

### Just Do It

Use this when the issue is obvious and the fix is simple.

Do not overanalyze what can be corrected immediately. If a small change removes friction and carries low risk, act on it. Anyone is empowered to call it out or take ownership and do it.

When in doubt, go to the Gemba: the place where the work actually happens. Obvious fixes reveal themselves most clearly when you observe the problem firsthand rather than from a distance.

### Kaizen

Use this when steady, incremental improvement is the right path.

Kaizen helps teams improve gradually through observation, small changes, and learning over time. It is useful when the process is fundamentally sound but needs ongoing refinement.

### Rootwork

Use this when the process no longer serves its purpose and must be rebuilt.

Rootwork is not optimization. It is radical reconstruction. It becomes necessary when the existing structure creates more drag than value and cannot be meaningfully repaired through incremental change.

## Roles in change

Change is carried by people, not presentations.

Mentors are ambassadors of the why. They carry the meaning and purpose of the change so others understand why it matters.

Leaders are ambassadors of the how. They ensure the change is translated into action, routines, accountability, and execution.

Mentors protect meaning. Leaders protect implementation.

## How this pillar connects

Process Improvement supports Culture and Engagement by reducing avoidable frustration and making it easier for people to do good work.

It strengthens Development because clearer systems create better learning environments. It enables Experimentation by lowering the cost of change. It improves Quality by reducing systemic errors and variation. It supports Prospective thinking by building systems capable of adapting without collapsing under complexity.

Without Process Improvement, effort gets trapped in waste. With it, effort becomes capacity.
