# Pillar 3: Process Improvement

## Philosophy

Process Improvement exists to save internal time and reinvest it in the customer experience.

The purpose of improving a process is to remove friction, reduce waste, increase clarity, and create more capacity for meaningful value. A strong process helps people do excellent work with less confusion, less rework, and less unnecessary effort.

## Why it matters

When internal systems are inefficient, the customer eventually pays for it.

The cost appears as delay, inconsistency, lower quality, frustration, burnout, or missed opportunity. Process Improvement is therefore not only an operational concern; it is a leadership responsibility.

## Core principles

- Save time internally to create more value externally.
- Design with scale in mind from the beginning. Be Scale-aware, not scale-obsessed.
- A garden is designed to grow, not to be fully grown from the start. Design with scale in mind but plant what the season calls for.
- Remove unnecessary friction before adding more control.
- Treat process improvement as a continuous capability, not a one-time project.
- Make problems visible before trying to solve them.

## Improvement methods

Kasvu uses an adaptive approach to improvement rather than a single prescribed doctrine.

### Just Do It

Use this when the issue is obvious and the fix is simple.

Do not overanalyze what can be corrected immediately. If a small change removes friction and carries low risk, act on it. Anyone is empowered to call it out or take ownership and do it.

When in doubt, go to the Gemba: the place where the work actually happens. Gemba is a Japanese term meaning "the actual place." In process improvement it means leaving your desk, your dashboard, and your assumptions behind and going to observe the work as it is actually performed. Obvious fixes reveal themselves most clearly when you watch the problem happen firsthand rather than hear about it secondhand.

Just Do It is not an excuse for carelessness. It is a bias against the kind of over-deliberation that lets obvious problems persist for months because no one wanted to act without a formal mandate. If the fix is clear, the risk is low, and the improvement is real — do it, tell someone, and move on.

### Kaizen

Use this when steady, incremental improvement is the right path.

Kaizen is a Japanese term meaning "change for the better." It describes a practice of continuous, incremental improvement driven by the people closest to the work. It is useful when the process is fundamentally sound but has accumulated friction, inconsistency, or small inefficiencies that compound over time.

Kaizen works through a simple cycle: observe the current process honestly, identify one small thing that could be better, change it, measure whether it improved, and repeat. The changes are small by design. The discipline is in the consistency — not a single improvement event, but a permanent habit of noticing and refining.

In Kasvu, Kaizen is not owned by a specialist or a separate improvement team. It belongs to the people doing the work. The leader's role is to create the conditions — time, safety, and visibility — for small improvements to surface and be acted on without requiring a formal process every time.


### Rootwork

Use this when the process no longer serves its purpose and must be rebuilt.

Rootwork is not optimization. It is radical reconstruction. It becomes necessary when the existing structure creates more drag than value and cannot be meaningfully repaired through incremental change.Patching a broken foundation is not improvement — it is delay.

Rootwork begins with honest diagnosis. Before rebuilding anything, the team must understand why the current process exists, what it was designed to do, and why it no longer does that well. Processes that look dysfunctional often made sense when they were designed. Rebuilding without that understanding tends to produce a new process with the same underlying problems.

Rootwork is slower and more disruptive than the other two methods. It should not be used lightly. But when a process has become the primary source of friction in a system — when people are working around it more than through it — incremental improvement is not honest. The root needs to come out.

### Choosing the right method

The three methods are not interchangeable. Using Kaizen on a broken foundation wastes effort. Using Rootwork on a process that just needs a small fix wastes time and creates unnecessary disruption.

A useful diagnostic before choosing:
- Can one person fix this in under an hour with no downstream risk? → Just Do It
- Is the process sound but accumulating friction over time? → Kaizen
- Are people consistently working around the process rather than through it? → Rootwork

## Roles in change

Change is carried by people, not presentations.

Mentors are ambassadors of the why. They carry the meaning and purpose of the change so others understand why it matters.

Leaders are ambassadors of the how. They ensure the change is translated into action, routines, accountability, and execution.

Mentors protect meaning. Leaders protect implementation.

## Signs this pillar is healthy

- People closest to the work feel empowered to flag and fix small problems without escalating everything.
- Processes exist because they create value, not because they have always existed.
- When something breaks repeatedly, the team asks what the system allowed rather than who caused it.
- Improvement is a continuous habit, not an annual event.
- Leaders can name the three or four processes most in need of attention right now.

## Signs this pillar needs attention

- The same problems recur despite repeated fixes.
- People have built workarounds that everyone uses but nobody has officially changed.
- Process documentation exists but does not reflect how work is actually done.
- Improvement projects are announced but rarely completed.
- Nobody knows which method to use when, so nothing gets improved systematically.

## How this pillar connects

Process Improvement supports Culture and Engagement by reducing avoidable frustration and making it easier for people to do good work.


It strengthens Development because clearer systems create better learning environments. It enables Experimentation by lowering the cost of change. It improves Quality by reducing systemic errors and variation. It supports Prospective thinking by building systems capable of adapting without collapsing under complexity.

Without Process Improvement, effort gets trapped in waste. With it, effort becomes capacity.
