# Pillar 4: Experimentation

## Philosophy

Experimentation is the disciplined practice of learning through action.

Kasvu fosters a culture of innate permission. People should not have to wait for excessive approval to test safe, thoughtful ideas that could improve the system. Action should not be reckless, but neither should the organization become paralyzed by permission-seeking.

Experimentation is how curiosity becomes progress.

## Why it matters

A system that cannot experiment becomes slow, defensive, and fragile.

When teams are discouraged from trying new approaches, the organization becomes dependent on habit, hierarchy, and hindsight. It reacts too late and learns too slowly.

Experimentation is a necessary condition for adaptation. It is how organizations discover better paths before they are forced into them.

## Core principles

- Action can precede permission when risk is contained and intent is constructive.
- Small, fast experiments are often more valuable than long theoretical debates.
- Learning matters more than being right.
- Not every idea should scale, but every serious experiment should teach something.
- Experimentation must be bounded by responsibility, not paralyzed by fear.

## The experimentation cycle

### 1. Quick filter

Start with a simple first test of whether the idea is worth pursuing.

Ask:
- What problem are we solving?
- Why does it matter?
- What would success look like?
- What is the smallest safe version of this test?

### 2. Agile pilot

Run a short, contained pilot focused on generating evidence quickly.

The goal is not a polished final solution. The goal is to learn enough to decide whether to continue, adapt, or stop. Keep the pilot small enough that stopping it costs nothing. The discipline is in resisting the urge to over-build before the evidence exists to justify it.

### 3. Dual measurement

Measure both hard results and lived experience.

Track KPIs, cycle time, quality, and customer outcome. Also pay attention to sentiment, usability, and behavioral response. A test may look efficient on paper while creating hidden resistance in reality.

### 4. Informed decision

At the end of every pilot, decide clearly:

- Scale it
- Refine it
- Pause it
- End it

An experiment is only useful if it leads to a decision and a lesson.

## Innate permission

The budget for thoughtful experimentation is considered innate to proactive behavior.

This does not mean unlimited spending or uncontrolled initiative. It means that responsible people should have enough trust, room, and support to test meaningful improvements without having to fight the system every time they want to learn.

Innate permission is not declared once and assumed forever. It is demonstrated repeatedly through how leaders respond when experiments are proposed and when they fail. A leader who grants permission in words but creates friction in practice has not created a culture of experimentation. They have created a culture of performance — people who look like they are experimenting while waiting to be told what to do.

A leader signals innate permission when they:
- Ask "what are you testing right now?" as often as "how are results?"
- Respond to a failed experiment with "what did we learn?" before anything else.
- Remove approval steps for experiments that meet a clear, pre-agreed threshold of risk and scope.
- Run experiments themselves and share the results regardless of outcome.

## How the organization embraces failure

A failed experiment is not a failed person. The distinction matters and must be maintained actively, not just stated.

When an experiment does not produce the hoped-for result, Kasvu asks one question before any other: was the experiment designed and run responsibly? If yes, the outcome is data — not a performance issue. If the design was careless or the risk was not contained, that is a separate conversation about judgment, not about the result.

Organizations that punish responsible failure do not eliminate experimentation. They drive it underground. People still try things — they just stop telling anyone when it does not work. That is the most dangerous version of an experimentation culture: one where learning disappears because honesty is not safe.

## Signs this pillar is healthy

- People can name an experiment they are currently running or recently completed.
- Failed experiments are discussed openly and without blame.
- The pathway from idea to test is short and does not require excessive approval.
- Results — positive and negative — are shared with the team, not buried.
- Leaders participate in experiments rather than only approving them.

## Signs this pillar needs attention

- Ideas are plentiful but nothing ever gets tested.
- Every experiment requires a proposal, a committee, and a roadmap slot before it can begin.
- Nobody can remember the last time something was tried and failed without consequence.
- Results are only shared when they are positive.
- The word innovation is used often but experimentation is rare.

## How this pillar connects

Experimentation energizes Culture and Engagement because people feel trusted to think and act.

It strengthens Development because people learn most deeply through practice and feedback. It depends on Process Improvement because better systems make testing easier and less disruptive. It sharpens Quality because experiments reveal what truly works for the customer. It is directed by Prospective thinking because the best experiments respond to emerging conditions, not only current pain.

Without Experimentation, growth becomes reactive. With it, growth becomes intentional.
