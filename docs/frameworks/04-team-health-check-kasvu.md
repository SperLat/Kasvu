# Team Health Check: The Kasvu Garden Audit

## Purpose

This framework gives a leader and their team a structured way to assess the current health of the system they are building together.

It is not a performance review. It is a honest look at how the six pillars are showing up in the team's daily reality: what is growing, what is stuck, and what needs tending.

## When to use it

Use this framework when:

- You want a shared picture of team health beyond engagement surveys.
- The team has been through a significant change and you want to take stock.
- You sense the team is drifting but cannot name exactly where.
- You want to involve the team in diagnosing their own system rather than having leadership diagnose it for them.
- You are starting a new leadership role and want to understand what you are inheriting.

Run it no more than once a quarter. More frequently and it loses its signal value.

## What you need

- 90 minutes with the full team.
- A safe environment where people feel free to be honest.
- A way to capture responses visibly: a whiteboard, shared doc, or digital canvas.

## The audit flow

### Step 1: Set the frame (5 minutes)

Before any questions, tell the team what this is and what it is not.

Something like: "This is not about performance. It is about the system we are all working in. I want us to look honestly at what is helping us grow and what is getting in our way. Everything said here stays in this room and feeds directly into how we work together going forward."

Mean it. If you are not prepared to act on what emerges, do not run this session.

### Step 2: Individual reflection (10 minutes)

Give each person 10 minutes to reflect privately before any group discussion.

Ask them to rate each pillar on a simple scale of 1 to 5, where 1 means "this is significantly broken for us" and 5 means "this is genuinely strong."

The six pillars to rate:

- Culture and Engagement: Do we trust each other? Do people feel safe to speak?
- Development: Are people growing? Is learning a real part of how we work?
- Process Improvement: Are our processes helping or getting in our way?
- Experimentation: Do people feel free to try things? Do we learn from what we test?
- Quality: Are we proud of what we produce? Does it actually serve the people we serve?
- Prospective: Are we paying attention to what is changing around us?

Ask them also to note one specific example for their lowest-rated pillar.

### Step 3: Share and surface (30 minutes)

Go through each pillar one at a time.

Ask for the ratings and write them visibly. Do not average them yet. The spread is more interesting than the mean.

For each pillar, ask: "Who gave this a low score and would be willing to say what they are seeing?"

Listen without defending. The leader's job in this step is to understand, not to explain.

### Step 4: Find the pattern (15 minutes)

After all six pillars have been discussed, step back and look at the whole picture together.

Ask the team:

- "What pattern do you see across these?"
- "Is there one pillar whose weakness is creating problems in the others?"
- "If we could only improve one thing in the next 90 days, what would have the most impact?"

Let the team answer these questions, not the leader.

### Step 5: Choose one thing to tend (20 minutes)

The team agrees on one pillar or one specific condition within a pillar to actively work on before the next audit.

Define it concretely:

- What does it look like right now?
- What would it look like if it were genuinely healthy?
- What is one specific change we will make to tend it?
- Who will take responsibility for keeping it visible?

Avoid the trap of trying to fix everything at once. One thing tended well creates more momentum than five things started and abandoned.

### Step 6: Close with appreciation (10 minutes)

End the session by asking each person to name one thing about the team they genuinely value.

This is not forced positivity. It is a deliberate act of grounding the team in what is worth protecting as they work on what needs to grow.

## Facilitator notes

The rating exercise is a starting point, not the output. What matters is the conversation the ratings open, not the numbers themselves.

If the team rates everything highly and the conversation stays surface-level, that is a signal about psychological safety, not a signal that everything is actually fine. Name it gently: "These are strong ratings. I want to make sure we are also giving space to the harder stuff. What is one thing you would say if you knew it would be received well?"

The leader should share their own ratings before asking others to share theirs. It sets the tone for honesty.

## Pillars activated

All six pillars are activated by design. This framework is a full-system health check.

## Suggested time

| Part | Time |
|---|---|
| Set the frame | 5 minutes |
| Individual reflection | 10 minutes |
| Share and surface | 30 minutes |
| Find the pattern | 15 minutes |
| Choose one thing to tend | 20 minutes |
| Close with appreciation | 10 minutes |
| **Total** | **90 minutes** |
