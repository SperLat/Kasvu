# Kasvu Glossary

This glossary defines the key terms, concepts, and frameworks used across the Kasvu philosophy and its supporting materials. When a term carries a specific meaning within Kasvu, that meaning takes precedence over its common usage.

---

## Core concepts
S
### Kasvu
The name of this philosophy. From the Finnish word for growth. In this context, growth refers not to scale or speed but to the natural unfolding of poStial when the right conditions are present.

### Cultivating
The leadership approach Kasvu advocates. Cultivating means working with the nature of what is already present: guiding, nurturing, and creating conditions for growth rather than imposing a predetermined form. Contrast with Sculpting.

### Sculpting
The leadership approach Kasvu challenges. Sculpting means imposing form from the outside, starting with a fixed image of perfection and removing everything that does not conform. It produces results that are precise but brittle, impressive but static. Contrast with Cultivating.

### Gardens, not statues
The central metaphor of Kasvu. Statues are forced into shape and cannot grow. Gardens are designed, tended, and allowed to become fully alive. Kasvu invites leaders to build organizations that function more like gardens than statues.

### Growth from within
The principle that lasting strength, resilience, and excellence emerge from conditions that are cultivated internally, not from forms that are imposed externally.

### The Penjing Master
The central figure of the Kasvu parable. She represents the cultivating leader: patient, observant, working with the nature of what she tends rather than against it. Her pine becomes resilient and alive in a way the jade dragon cannot.

### The Imperial Sculptor
The contrasting figure in the Kasvu parable. He represents the sculpting leader: precise, obedient to a fixed vision, producing results that are impressive but static. His jade dragon is flawless but cannot grow.

### Ying Ke Song
The name given to the Penjing Master's pine in the parable. A real tree in China said to be more than one thousand years old, shaped by wind and adversity into a form of unrepeatable character. In Kasvu it represents what resilience looks like when it is cultivated over time.

## The six pillars

### Culture and Engagement
Pillar 1. The foundation of Kasvu. Built on trust, psychological safety, and high standards. The soil in which every other pillar grows.

### Development
Pillar 2. The discipline of growing people intentionally so the system gains strength over time. Favors cascading mentorship and the Echo Effect.

### Process Improvement
Pillar 3. The practice of removing friction and reinvesting saved time in the customer experience. Uses adaptive methods: Just Do It, Kaizen, and Rootwork.

### Experimentation
Pillar 4. The disciplined practice of learning through action. Builds a culture of innate permission so people can test ideas and generate evidence without excessive approval.

### Quality
Pillar 5. The standard of excellence defined from the customer's perspective. Protected through shared ownership and a dual model of team responsibility and structural reinforcement.

### Prospective
Pillar 6. The practice of treating the future as a daily habit. Builds anticipation into the rhythm of the organization so that change is met with readiness rather than reaction.

## Frameworks and methods

### K.A.S.V.U. framework
The operating tenets within Pillar 1. A behavioral model for service-oriented and leadership environments. Each letter represents a principle: Know the Core Need, Answer with Heart, Seize the Opportunity, Vow for Excellence, Understand the Unasked Question.

### Echo Effect
A concept within Pillar 2. Describes the multiplier impact of development: one person grows, then helps others grow, and over time the organization gains depth rather than just volume.

### Just Do It
An improvement method within Pillar 3. Used when the issue is obvious and the fix is simple. Act immediately rather than overanalyzing.

### Kaizen
An improvement method within Pillar 3. Steady, incremental improvement through observation, small changes, and learning over time. Used when the process is sound but needs refinement.

### Rootwork
An improvement method within Pillar 3. Radical reconstruction of a process that no longer serves its purpose. Used when incremental improvement and Kaizen are insufficient. Unlike pruning or tending, Rootwork means going all the way down to the source of the problem and rebuilding from there.

### Innate permission
A concept within Pillar 4. The idea that responsible people should not need excessive approval to test thoughtful, low-risk ideas. The budget for experimentation is considered inherent to proactive behavior.

### The experimentation cycle
A structured approach within Pillar 4 for moving from idea to decision. Four stages: Quick Filter, Agile Pilot, Dual Measurement, Informed Decision.

### Dual measurement
A practice within the experimentation cycle. Measuring both hard results such as KPIs and lived experience such as sentiment and behavioral response to get a complete picture of what a test actually produced.

### Post-mortem
A failure analysis practice within Pillar 5. A structured review that examines what happened, why it happened, and what must change. The purpose is learning and prevention, not punishment.

### The Root Reading
Kasvu's named practice for failure analysis within Pillar 5. A structured act of collective inquiry into what the system produced and why. Unlike a standard post-mortem, a Root Reading looks beyond the immediate failure to examine the health of the system that produced it, including conditions external to the grove itself. Reserved for significant failures, recurring patterns, and near-misses that reveal something meaningful about systemic health.

### The Grove
A recurring gathering where leadership and front-line teams come together to read the environment collectively. Named for a gathering of different trees, where older ones protect newer ones as they learn to read the wind and lean with it rather than fight against it. The Grove thinks of time as one, relying on past experiences to teach how to lean into the breeze. Both forms — Open Grove and Structured Grove — carry the same purpose: to grow stronger and with purpose.

### Open Grove
The informal, unrestricted form of The Grove. Any member of the team can bring what they are seeing without agenda or hierarchy. Run as often as the team needs it.

### Structured Grove
The formal form of The Grove within a mentorship program. Held on a regular cadence with intentional facilitation and continuity across sessions.

### Gemba
From Japanese, meaning "the real place." The practice of going to where the work actually happens to observe and understand problems firsthand. Used within the Just Do It method in Pillar 3 to ground immediate action in direct observation rather than assumption.

## Leadership concepts

### The gardener's role
The model of leadership Kasvu advocates. The leader as gardener creates and maintains the conditions in which others can grow into their own strength. The gardener does not produce the outcome directly; the gardener cultivates the conditions that make the outcome possible.

### Blame the process, not the person
A principle within Pillar 1. When something fails, the first question is what in the system allowed this to happen, not who caused it. This protects psychological safety while still demanding accountability for improvement.

### Cascading mentorship
A development model within Pillar 2. Those who grow are expected to help others grow. Creates a multiplier effect where development spreads through the system rather than concentrating in a few individuals.

### Ambassadors of the why
A role concept within Pillar 3. Mentors who carry the meaning and purpose of change so others understand why it matters. Distinct from ambassadors of the how.

### Ambassadors of the how
A role concept within Pillar 3. Leaders who translate change into action, routines, accountability, and execution. Distinct from ambassadors of the why.

### Strategic lag
A risk concept within Pillar 6. What happens when teams are consumed only by the present and become blind to emerging conditions. Prospective thinking exists to prevent strategic lag.

### Answer the unanswered question
A principle shared across Pillar 1 and Pillar 6. The practice of noticing what has not yet been asked but already matters, and addressing it proactively.

## Guiding questions

These questions appear throughout the Kasvu philosophy as orientation points for leaders and teams.

- "Are we architecting a garden, or just shaping a statue?"
- "What is the market now, and where is it heading?"
- "Blame the process, not the person."
- "You only need one tree to create the genesis of a forest."
- "You asked the Sculptor to replicate greatness. I only cultivated resilience. Greatness was the consequence."
